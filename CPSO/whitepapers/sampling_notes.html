Overview of Spectral Approach for DoE

Overview

Here I am taking notes on Spectral Approach for the Design of Experiments: Design, Analysis and Algorithms, by Kailkhura et. al,

Definitions

Definition 1: Space-filling Design

A space-filling design is a set of samples that are distributed according to a uniform probability distribution (Objective 1: Randomness) but no two samples are closer than a given minimum distance \(r_{\text{min}}\) (Objective 2: Coverage).

Definition 2: Point-Correlation Function (PCF)

Denote the intensity of a point process \(\mathcal{X}\) as \(\lambda(\mathcal{X})\), which is the average number of points in an infinitesimal volume around \(\mathcal{X}\) . For isotropic point processes (invariant under rotation / independent of direction) this is a constant value. To define the product density \(\beta\), let \(\{B_i\}\) denote the set of infinitesimal spheres around the points, and \(\{\text{d}V_i\}\) indicate the volume measures of \(B_i\) . Then we have \(\mathbb{P}(\mathbf{X}_1 = \mathbf{x}_1, \dots ,\mathbf{X}_N = \mathbf{x}_N) = \beta(\mathbf{x}_1, \dots , \mathbf{x}_N)\text{d}V_1\dots\text{d}V_N\) which represents the probability of having points \(\{\mathbf{x}_i\}\) in infinitesimal spheres \(\{B_i\}\). In the isotropic case, for a pair of points, \(\beta\) depends only on the distance between the points. Thus, \(\beta(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) = \beta(\|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|) = \beta(r)\) and \(\mathbb{P}(r) = \beta(r)\text{d}V_i\text{d}V_j\) . The PCF is then defined as \[ G(r) = \frac{\beta}{\lambda^2} \]

The authors note that the PCF characterizes the spatial properties of a sample design. However, it is easier to link the quality metric of a sample design to its spectral properties. Therefore, they establish a connection between the spatial and spectral properties of a sample design.

Definition 3: Power Spectral Density

For a finite set of \(N\) points, \(\{\mathbf{x}_j\}_{j = 1}^N\) in a region with unit volume, the power spectral density of the sampling function \(\sum_{j = 1}^N \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j)\) , where $\delta$ is a delta function, is formally defined as:

\[ P(\mathbf{k}) = \frac{1}{N}|S(\mathbf{k})|^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j, \ell}\exp\big(-2 \pi i \mathbf{k}.(\mathbf{x}_\ell - \mathbf{x}_j) \big) \]

Where \(|\cdot|\) is the \(\ell_2\) -norm and \(S(\mathbf{k})\) denotes the Fourier transform of the sampling function.

Simple Example: Definition 3

Let’s have a simple example, where \(N = 2\) and \(\{\mathbf{x}_j\}_{j = 1}^N = \big\{ (0,0), (0.5,0.5)\}\). The sampling function is then \(\sum_{j = 1}^N \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_j) = \delta(\mathbf{x} - (0,0)) + \delta(\mathbf{x} - (0.5,0.5))\). The Fourier transform is then \[S(\mathbf{k}) = \sum_{j=1}^2 \exp(-2i\pi \mathbf{k}\cdot \mathbf{x}_j) = e^{- 2 i \pi \mathbf{k} \cdot (0,0)} + e^{- 2 i \pi \mathbf{k} \cdot (0.5,0.5)}\] If we let \(\mathbf{k} = (k_1, k_2)\) then \[ S(\mathbf{k})=e^{- 2 i \pi (k_1, k_2) \cdot (0,0)} + e^{- 2 i \pi (k_1, k_2) \cdot (0.5,0.5)} = 1 + e^{- i \pi (k_1 + k_2)} \] Then, \(P(\mathbf{k}) = \frac{1}{N}|S(\mathbf{k})|^2\) can be written as: \[ \frac{1}{2}\bigg|1 + e^{-i\pi (k_1 + k_2)^2}\bigg|^2 = \frac{1}{2}\bigg( (1 + e^{-i \pi(k_1 + k_2)})(1 + e^{i \pi (k_1 + k_2)})\bigg) = \frac{1}{2}\Big(2 + 2\cos\big(\pi(k_1 + k_2)\big)\Big) \] Which, using \(1 + \cos(x) = 2 \cos^2(x/2)\) simplifies to… \[ P(\mathbf{k}) = 2\cos^2\Big(\frac{\pi}{2}(k_1 + k_2) \Big) \] Which we notice is equivalent to the written equation… \[ P(\mathbf{k}) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i,j}\exp\big(-2 i \pi \mathbf{k} \cdot (\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j)\big) \] The above evaluates to the binomial expansion shown previously.
